I’m going to specifically explore how outcomes for dogs and cats have changed over time.

We’ can see ’re focusing on the animal_outcomes.csv file, which has columns for the year, animal, and outcome in question, and then the number of cases matching that criteria in each of the Australian states.

The first thing we’ll do is transform this into a long-form dataset, creating a “State” column and a “Count” column, rather than keeping this “wide” dataset:

# Pivot data to long form
animal_outcomes_long=animal_outcomes%>%
  pivot_longer(-c(year,animal_type,outcome),names_to = "State",values_to="Count")

It looks like a couple of the outcomes - “In Stock” and “Currently In Care” - are non-definitive. In other words, we still don’t know what will happen to these animals in the longer term. So when considering outcomes, we’ll remove these cases from our analysis.

What I’d love to understand is how the outcomes break down in terms of positive outcomes - i.e. cases that didn’t result in the animal being Euthanized.

Let’s pull together a summary table, showing how each state performed for each animal group in each year, in terms of positive outcome %, along with a few other key stats.

# Group by state, animal, and year
state_totals=animal_outcomes_long%>%
  group_by(State,year,animal_type)%>%
  summarise(Total=sum(Count),
            Outcome_Achieved=sum(Count[!outcome %in% c("In Stock","Currently In Care")]),
            Euthanized=sum(Count[outcome=="Euthanized"]),
            Reclaimed=sum(Count[outcome=="Reclaimed"]),
            Rehomed=sum(Count[outcome=="Rehomed"]),
            Outcome_Not_Reclaimed=sum(Count[!outcome %in% c("In Stock","Currently In Care","Reclaimed")]))%>%
  mutate(Positive_Outcome=1-round(Euthanized/Outcome_Achieved,3),
         Reclaim_Prop=round(Reclaimed/Outcome_Achieved,3),
         Rehome_Prop=round(Rehomed/Outcome_Not_Reclaimed,3))%>%
  select(State,year,animal_type,Total,Outcome_Achieved,Positive_Outcome,Reclaim_Prop,Rehome_Prop)

Now the data is in a format for our visualisation. We can see the proportion of positive outcomes per animal/state/year combination.

Let’s visualise the total positive outcome rates over time for cats and dogs.

# Filter to focus on dogs and cats only
dogs_cats=state_totals%>%
  filter(animal_type %in% c("Dogs","Cats"))

# Visualise total positive outcome rate for dogs and cats by year
dogs_cats%>%
  filter(State=="Total")%>%
  ggplot(aes(x=factor(year),y=Positive_Outcome,col=animal_type))+
  geom_line(aes(group=animal_type),size=1)+
  geom_point(size=2)+
  theme_fivethirtyeight()+
  theme(axis.title = element_text(size=10,colour=Text_1),

We can see that dogs have consistently seen more positive outcomes than cats over this period. However, the gap has narrowed significantly in the past decade, and both dogs and cats have seen improvements in outcomes.

However, it would be interesting to understand why the gap exists. One potential factor might be to do with reclaim rates, as shown in the chart below:

We can see from this that cats are far less likely to be reclaimed than dogs, and that this could be a key reason for their lower positive outcome rate. There could be a number of reasons for this - cats are more likely to stray further from their home than dogs, so it may be harder to track down the owners of those picked up by the RSPCA.

Some of the other states might benefit from reviewing the approaches taken in these areas to improving outcomes.
